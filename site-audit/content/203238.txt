<h4 class="wp-block-heading has-text-align-center has-primary-color has-text-color has-link-color">Dry, hard summer ground making your horse tender? Dealing with abscesses, white line disease, or prepping for a trim?</h4>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:spacer {"height":"13px"} -->
<div style="height:13px" aria-hidden="true" class="wp-block-spacer"></div>

<!-- wp:columns -->

<div class="wp-block-column"><!-- wp:embed {"url":"https://youtu.be/CgFA5KAmy-o","type":"video","providerNameSlug":"youtube","responsive":true,"className":"wp-embed-aspect-16-9 wp-has-aspect-ratio"} -->

<!-- wp:column -->
<div class="wp-block-column"><!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><strong><em>By Cavallo Customer Experience Expert Maren</em></strong>:</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Ah, summer: long days, sunshine, birds chirping… and hooves hard as rocks! While hard, dry hooves are great, sometimes you do need to soak your horse’s hooves, such as sensitivity on hard, dry ground, or when dealing with an abscess, white line disease and medicated soaks, or just softening things up before a trim (your trimmer will thank you – trust me!).</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>But let’s be honest here. Soaking hooves can be a soggy disaster!!! Buckets get kicked, water goes everywhere, and somehow you end up wetter than the horse. Sound familiar? Luckily for you, your Cavallo boots can double as soaking boots with just a few, easy adjustments.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Cover the side drainage holes with duct tape (don’t forget the little slits at the back!). To hold moisture even better, you can add a small towel inside the boot soles, just where you would put your hoof boot pad. If you need to soak above the cornet band, simply slip a bread bag over the foot, pop the boot on, and fill it up. Easy, effective, and bucket-free. Your horse stays happy, and you stay dry… mostly!&nbsp;</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>–<em>Maren</em></p>

<p>We’re often asked if Cavallo Boots can keep up with the extra motion of gaited horses. This video says it all!</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Gaited Horse Trainer Ivy Starnes trusts Cavallo Trek Boots during training sessions, and her horses move fluidly, showing how well the boots stay secure and in sync with each step.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Love gaited horses and curious about gait differences? Ivy explains what makes a single foot gait as well as what gaits it’s commonly confused with.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>“The sound will still be the same: pucka, pucka, pucka, pucka.”</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>“Both are evenly timed gaits with no head nod, but it’s about how many feet are on the ground that determines which movement it is.”</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>She adds, “In my opinion, after a lot of research, the singlefoot gait is the same as the speed rack and fast ‘tölt’. It is an evenly timed movement, with no head nod. It has a moment when one foot is on the ground, and three are in the air. Which follows by two feet on the ground and two in the air, and it repeats.”</p>
