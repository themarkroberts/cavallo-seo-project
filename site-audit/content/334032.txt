<h4 class="wp-block-heading has-text-align-center">Snow Happens. Ice Balls Are Optional ?</h4>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:group {"layout":{"type":"constrained"}} -->
<div class="wp-block-group"><!-- wp:group {"layout":{"type":"constrained"}} -->
<div class="wp-block-group"><!-- wp:group {"style":{"dimensions":{"minHeight":"0px"},"border":{"width":"14px"}},"borderColor":"white","layout":{"type":"flex","flexWrap":"nowrap","verticalAlignment":"top"}} -->
<div class="wp-block-group has-border-color has-white-border-color" style="border-width:14px;min-height:0px"><!-- wp:image {"className":"is-style-default","style":{"border":{"radius":{"topLeft":"12px","topRight":"12px","bottomLeft":"12px","bottomRight":"12px"}},"layout":{"selfStretch":"fixed","flexSize":"1200px"}}} -->
<figure class="wp-block-image has-custom-border is-style-default"><img src="http://cavallo.local/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/Ice-Balling-Metal-horseshoes-Snow-Sue-Grice.jpg" alt="Ice Balling - Metal horseshoes Snow Sue Grice" style="border-top-left-radius:12px;border-top-right-radius:12px;border-bottom-left-radius:12px;border-bottom-right-radius:12px" title="Ice Balling – Metal horseshoes Snow Sue Grice"/></figure>
<!-- /wp:image -->

<!-- wp:group {"layout":{"type":"constrained"}} -->
<div class="wp-block-group"><!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Ice balling is one of those Winter problems that have been accepted for so long, it rarely gets questioned. Wet snow packs into a metal horseshoe, melts on contact, then refreezes into a hard, slippery mass with every step. The result is an unstable, high-heeled block of ice that compromises balance, increases the risk of slips and falls, and adds unnecessary strain to tendons and ligaments. It’s not a rare fluke of winter weather… It’s a predictable combination of cold metal, frozen moisture, and pressure.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Preventing ice balls usually means managing the shoe rather than the snow. Farriers may add anti-snowball, bubble, or rim pads.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Temporary fixes like petroleum jelly or cooking spray can help in a pinch, but they wear off quickly. Cavallo Hoof boots offer a practical alternative for both barefoot AND shod horses during turnout or riding, while improving traction. And for horses, going barefoot in the Winter months can dramatically reduce ice accumulation. Which raises the question so many horse owners ask themselves each year: if ice balling is caused by metal shoes, why keep the metal when there are ways to avoid the problem entirely?</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
