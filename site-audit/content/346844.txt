<h2 class="wp-block-heading">What are Hoof Cracks, and What Causes Them?</h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Hoof cracks are vertical or sometimes horizontal splits in the hoof wall that can range from narrow, superficial lines to deep fractures that compromise internal hoof structures. Some cracks are cosmetic and harmless, while others extend into sensitive tissue and cause pain, instability, or lameness. </p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Vertical cracks are the most common type and typically follow the direction of the hoof tubules in the wall. Consequently, these types of cracks are often caused by stress on the hoof wall and dietary imbalances. Horizontal cracks often result from trauma or an abscess that has ruptured at the coronary band.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The outer hoof wall bears weight and protects the internal foot structures while growing continuously from the coronary band. When mechanical forces overwhelm the wall’s strength or the horn quality itself is weakened, cracks can develop.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph --></div>
<!-- /wp:column -->

<div class="wp-block-uagb-advanced-heading uagb-block-0d402799"><h2 class="uagb-heading-text">Key Factors That Can Contribute to Hoof Cracks</h2></div>
<!-- /wp:uagb/advanced-heading -->

<!-- wp:image {"id":346810,"width":"465px","height":"auto","aspectRatio":"5.953722930881044","sizeSlug":"large","linkDestination":"none","align":"center"} -->
<figure class="wp-block-image aligncenter size-large is-resized"><img src="http://cavallo.local/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Cavallo-Case-Studies-2026-Website-Cover-4-1024x172.jpg" alt="Cavallo Hoof Boots for Riding, Therapy, Turnout, and more" class="wp-image-346810" style="aspect-ratio:5.953722930881044;width:465px;height:auto"/></figure>
<!-- /wp:image -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li><strong>Hoof imbalance and conformational stress.</strong> Long toes, low heels, sheared heels, and uneven landing patterns concentrate force in specific areas of the wall, making cracks more likely. Horses with certain conformations or genetics (e.g., thin walls) are more prone to cracking.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li><strong>Improper trimming or shoeing.</strong> Further, infrequent trims, poor breakover, or shoes that don’t support the hoof can shift weight distribution and strain the wall.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li><strong>Trauma and abscesses.</strong> Blows to the hoof wall or coronary band, or old abscess tracts, can initiate cracks that migrate downward as the hoof grows.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li><strong>Environment.</strong> Wet conditions soften and weaken the horn, while dry conditions can make the hoof horn brittle. Rapid swings between wet and dry conditions increase stress and the risk of cracking.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li><strong>Nutrition and hoof quality.</strong> Diets low in essential amino acids, biotin, zinc, copper, and other trace minerals result in weaker horns that chip or crack.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">How Cavallo Hoof Boots Support Hoof Crack Treatment and Healing</h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Hoof cracks heal only through new growth from the coronary band, but protection during that process is essential. Cavallo Hoof Boots provide a cushioned barrier that shields compromised hoof walls from concussion, uneven terrain, and further tearing. By reducing mechanical stress and minimizing leverage at breakover, boots help stabilize cracks during healing.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Comfort also plays a key role. Horses with sore feet move less, which limits circulation to the hoof capsule. Cavallo boots improve comfort on firm ground, encouraging natural movement and supporting healthy horn production. Removable pads allow added cushioning and pressure distribution for sensitive or rehabilitating hooves.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>While boots do not directly heal the crack itself, they create a safer, more controlled environment that supports sound, comfortable recovery.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Troubleshooting Hoof Cracks: Assessment and Intervention</h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Firstly, successful management starts by identifying the type and severity of the crack. Superficial cracks that run only through the outer wall often remain cosmetic and may simply grow out with regular, balanced trimming. However, cracks that widen under load, show signs of infection, or cause heat and sensitivity should be evaluated by a farrier and veterinarian, as they signal deeper involvement.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Addressing the underlying imbalance and cause is the most effective long-term strategy. As a result, consistent trimming that restores balanced landing and proper breakover and reduces stress on the hoof wall is vital.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Management and environment are also essential. Keeping a <strong>consistent trim schedule</strong>, avoiding constant exposure to extreme wet or dry conditions, and promoting circulation through turnout and moderate exercise all support healthy growth.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Furthermore, nutrition must be reviewed. Adequate protein, biotin, zinc, copper, and other micronutrients are building blocks for a strong hoof horn. Consequently, without them, cracks are more likely to recur even with excellent trimming.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Healing takes time. A hoof wall defect often requires months of growth to replace fully, so patience and ongoing maintenance are critical for lasting improvement.</p>
