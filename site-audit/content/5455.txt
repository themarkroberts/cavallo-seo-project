<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>At Cavallo, we assist horses by providing comfort and protection. We serve rescues, therapy organizations and horse owners by alleviating the pain of lameness. We help with educational initiatives, scientific research, behaviour and wellness studies, disaster relief funds, and emotional support for seniors, sick children, and PTSD and abuse victims. We support keeping the horse and human bond intact through riding associations, riding groups and training programs.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Ebony Horsewomen Therapeutic Riding</h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>In 1984, Patricia E. Kelly, a U.S. Marine Corp Vietnam-era veteran and African-American cowgirl, founded Ebony Horsewomen, Inc. Her inspiration for starting EHI came from an inner-city child who asked her, “is that a real horse?” Her dream was to level the playing field for inner-city youth, to give them access to the same opportunities, resources, equine-based programming, and equine-assisted therapy as their suburban counterparts. EHI has become the regional leader in providing Culturally Competent Equine Assisted Therapy and Psychotherapy. Mental health professionals, MSW, and BSW students can earn Continuing Education Credits (CECs) approved by NASW/CT.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Andy Robinson, Working Animals Supervisor – Weald &amp; Downland Living Museum</h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>For Andy Robinson, a big part of his job at the Weald &amp; Downland Living Museum is working the draft horses on the farm and around the Museum site. They farm the land as much as possible, using traditional methods to represent how rural communities in the past would have worked. The historical period they cover at the Museum means tractors were only just in their infancy, so they rely on horses and horse-drawn mechanisms, just as our ancestors did. Cavallo donates BFB Boots &amp; Buckle Straps to Andy, to assist his horses when needed.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Border Horse Slaughter Rescue</h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>In the United States, countless unwanted, abandoned, and sick horses end up in kill pens until they’re shipped to slaughter in Mexico. <h2 class="wp-block-heading">Bear Valley Rescue</h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>In 2004, Bear Valley Rescue in Alberta became involved in rescue when they learned about PMU (Pregnant Mare’s Urine) farms. The PMU industry started with the advent of hormone replacement therapy for women. These farms bred mares to collect their urine during pregnancy. A downturn in the industry occurred due to concerns about cancer in women taking PMU drugs, and Bear Valley started buying pregnant mares that were being culled. The rest is history. The rescue now has about 150 horses in care and has found wonderful homes for many more.</p>
