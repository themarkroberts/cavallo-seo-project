<h4><strong>It's All About Survival</strong></h4>
Without good feet, horses do not survive. They are prey animals, and if they cannot run away, they do not make it. While there are no crouching tigers around, ready to pounce, paying attention to healthy hooves is as essential today as it ever was. It may be even more imperative because of the domesticity and human intervention, which can compromise natural hoof function.
<h4><strong>Determining the Presence of White Line</strong></h4>
A common hoof problem is <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_line_disease">White Line Disease</a>. The White Line is the material between the sole and the wall, which can be more clearly seen after a fresh trim. One theory is that it is an invasive fungus that enters the area through the sole. Also, bacteria may enter as a result of injury and damage to the hoof wall. It could also be a result of chronic <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laminitis">Laminitis</a> or a metabolic disturbance involving ageing. Different hypotheses aside, we know that the white line material is unhealthy.

Investigate by cleaning and examining the hoof. Look for signs of trauma, sores, disturbances, or openings in the hoof wall and sole. If something looks questionable, treat it. Start with natural remedies that are non-toxic with no adverse side effects. Apple cider vinegar, iodine and tea tree oil can be helpful. White Line Disease is an <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaerobic_organism">anaerobic condition</a>, thriving in the absence of oxygen. When applying a treatment, you should make sure the area also receives clean air exposure.
<h4>Keep that Horse Moving!</h4>
After you have treated the symptom, look for the original cause. Your trimmer should balance the hoof from front to back, side to side and be able to address the rigour required of a hoof that supports a thousand-pound horse.

Movement is imperative. The nutrients and oxygen in freely flowing blood can help heal problems and prevent further damage.  Proper circulation helps to encourage the hoof strength and elasticity required to support your horse from the ground up.  Keep him comfortable and keep him moving. If your horse does not respond quickly, call your veterinarian.
