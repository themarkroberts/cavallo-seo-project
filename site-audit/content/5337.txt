<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="http://cavallo.local/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Western-Kentucky-University-Hoof-Boot-Study-Cavallo-2.jpg" alt="" class="wp-image-5339"/></figure>

<!-- wp:column {"verticalAlignment":"center","width":"80%"} -->
<div class="wp-block-column is-vertically-aligned-center" style="flex-basis:80%"><!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Dr. Jennifer Gill and Gabriella Lynn of the Western Kentucky University Equine Science Department completed the first independent university study comparing hoof boots to traditional metal shoes. They chose their preferred boots for the study – the Cavallo Trek. </p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Results confirmed that Cavallo Hoof Boots benefit horses, provide comfort, protection and minimize potential hoof damage. The study focused on surface area, force, and pressure exerted on horses’ hooves while travelling on the hard surface of asphalt. The Scientists used FujiFilm imaging to determine the amount of pressure felt in the horse’s hooves, which allowed the scientists to see dynamic pressure through colour mapping. </p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph --></div>
<!-- /wp:column --></div>
<!-- /wp:columns -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><strong>From Dr. Jennifer Gill:</strong>&nbsp;<em>“We found that the Cavallo Trek Hoof Boots were significantly lower in pressure than the other two treatments,”</em>&nbsp;Gill said.&nbsp;<em>“And this is what we expected from our data. We would have expected that the Cavallo Trek Hoof Boots would have been lower, especially with hard terrain. This was good news for what we were expecting to see.”</em></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph --></div>
<!-- /wp:group -->

<!-- wp:group {"layout":{"type":"constrained"}} -->
<div class="wp-block-group"><!-- wp:columns -->
<div class="wp-block-columns"><!-- wp:column {"verticalAlignment":"center","width":"20%"} -->
<div class="wp-block-column is-vertically-aligned-center" style="flex-basis:20%"><!-- wp:image {"id":5340,"sizeSlug":"full","linkDestination":"none"} -->
<figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img src="http://cavallo.local/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Western-Kentucky-University-Logo-452x441-1.webp" alt="" class="wp-image-5340"/></figure>
<!-- /wp:image --></div>
<!-- /wp:column -->

<!-- wp:column {"verticalAlignment":"center","width":"80%"} -->
<div class="wp-block-column is-vertically-aligned-center" style="flex-basis:80%"><!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>“<em>The Cavallo Boots protected the entire hoof itself, which we thought was very beneficial, especially over some other types of hoof protection, such as horseshoes,”</em>&nbsp;Gill said.&nbsp;<em>“We also found that the tread of the Cavallo Boots was thicker and would provide more space to contact with the ground than a metal horseshoe. The Trek Boots were significantly lower in pressure than the other two treatments.&nbsp;The contacting surface area with the Trek Boots was significantly higher than the other two treatments. There was more tread that was in contact with the ground than would be with the steel horseshoe and a steel horseshoe with Equi-Pak.”&nbsp;</em>&nbsp;<em>“Based on this result, we would say that horses would have better traction in the Cavallo Trek Hoof Boots and be better able to dissipate those forces and pressure because of the increases in surface area.”</em></p>

When I started asking, “Why would this happy, beautiful, 14-year-old horse have so many problems?”, it drove me crazy to be told that this is the way it is with horses, and I should get used to it.</p>

I would give them stall rest, or change the exercise program, or change the farrier, or the feed, or the supplements, but sure enough sooner or later – I’d go out to the barn and there it would be again – maybe not full-on lameness this time, but the peaked eye lid, the swishing tail, the inability to pick up the correct lead.
